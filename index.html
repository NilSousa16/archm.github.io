1. Introduction

Smart Cities can be defined as urban centers that utilize digital data-driven technologies to optimize public services and increase resource use efficiency. A Smart City is composed of six main components: smart governance, smart economy, smart citizens, smart mobility, smart environment, and smart quality of life.

As Internet of Things (IoT) technologies advance, Smart Cities benefit from these innovations by providing more efficient services and optimizing resource usage. Generally, IoT is oriented towards technological infrastructure, while Smart Cities focus on meeting users' needs. However, both share the goal of improving urban services.

The implementation of IoT in Smart Cities, as in other contexts, is associated with the use of an extensive hardware infrastructure composed of heterogeneous devices. This diversity is necessary for monitoring various environments, such as parks, parking lots, buildings, traffic, and atmospheric conditions. For example, in a park, specific sensors may be employed to monitor humidity, temperature, gases in the air, the presence of people, and the control of public lighting. This scenario highlights the significant number of devices required in IoT infrastructures for Smart City solutions.

Another relevant aspect is the heterogeneity of IoT infrastructures, which involves a wide variety of devices and communication protocols. Each device may have different processing capabilities and adopt varying communication standards, resulting in a lack of standardization across solutions. Combined with the large volume of devices, the management, monitoring, and maintenance of the availability and efficiency of these infrastructures become a significant challenge.

In light of this, to ensure the availability and proper functioning of devices and the IoT infrastructure, remote monitoring and control of these devices are essential without overburdening operational resources. This enables the continuous operation of various services offered by Smart Cities and ensures the high availability of the IoT infrastructure.

To achieve this goal, the development of management mechanisms capable of handling the specific characteristics of IoT infrastructures in the context of Smart Cities becomes necessary. This development must take into account the particularities and complexities of these environments. The creation of a dedicated architecture for device management can significantly contribute to ensuring the proper functioning and maintenance of these infrastructures.

1.1 Goal

This document aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the proposed architecture for managing IoT infrastructures in the context of Smart Cities, termed Integrated Architecture for IoT Device Management in Smart Cities (ArchM).

1.2 Scope

The architecture described in this document is applicable to the management of IoT devices across various technological solutions aimed at Smart Cities. The utilization of its principles and methods will contribute to the organization and standardization of IoT device management processes in different scenarios. Furthermore, the adoption of ArchM will enable managerial integration and collaboration among multiple solutions, fostering greater efficiency and interoperability.

1.3 Definitions, Acronyms and Abbreviations

This section presents some of the most common terms and their meanings, which are used in the architecture description.

  • Integrated Architecture for IoT Device Management in Smart Cities (ArchM) - Architecture for IoT Device Management in the Context of Smart Cities.

  • Composition of Solutions Smart Cities or Smart Composition (SC) - Refers to a set of Smart Solutions (SS) managed collectively as if they were a single Smart Solution.

1.4 Architecture Requirements

The Integrated Architecture for IoT Device Management in Smart Cities is designed for managing IoT infrastructures in solutions implemented in Smart Cities. This architecture provides a framework for the development and enhancement of IoT device management systems. Its development began with a requirements gathering process that identified the needs to be addressed by the architecture.

To ensure a clear and consistent view of the architecture, high-level requirements are specified according to the following elements:

  • Identifier: represents the unique ID and title of the requirement;
  • Name: short title of the requirement;
  • Description: provides a summary of the functionality;
  • Impact: discusses the relevance of the requirement to the architecture;

2. Architectural Representation

The Integrated Architecture for IoT Device Management in Smart Cities (ArchM) is an architecture described in this document, aimed at enabling the development of platforms for managing devices that make up the infrastructure of Internet of Things (IoT) solutions in the context of Smart Cities.

Figura 1 - Overview of Infrastructure in Smart Cities

In Figure 1, it can be observed that a Smart City is composed of various distinct solutions, which often operate in isolation. Each of these solutions has its own characteristics, such as types and quantities of devices, communication protocols, running software, and data storage structures.

In this context, ArchM organizes management into two parts: the Smart City Solution, or simply Smart Solution (SS), and the Composition of Smart Cities Solutions, or Smart Composition (SC). SS refers to a specific solution for a Smart City, such as traffic monitoring on a highway, managed in isolation from ArchM perspective. On the other hand, SC represents a set of SS, meaning a group of Smart City solutions that are managed as a single solution.

2.1 Management Requirements

ArchM must meet certain management requirements, considered essential for the effective management of IoT infrastructures in Smart Cities. For this purpose, the ISO/IEC 30141:2018 and ISO/IEC 20924:2018 standards were used in defining these requirements. The requirements are as follows:

  • Separation of Functional and Management Capabilities: All management resources must operate independently of functional resources.
  • Heterogeneity: Management must be prepared to handle devices and systems with diverse characteristics.
  • Highly Distributed Systems: Management must be capable of handling the high distribution of devices and systems that may exist.
  • Scalability: Management must be maintained even as the number of devices connected to IoT systems increases.
  • Resource Sharing: The manager must utilize resource sharing among devices and systems to ensure the efficient operation of the entire infrastructure.

2.2 Architectural Entities

This section presents the entities that are part of the scope covered by ArchM. In this context, architectural entities refer to the components present in IoT solutions that may be part of the set of elements involved in management, either as managed elements or as managing elements.

These elements were defined based on the ISO/IEC 30141:2018 standard, which presents a reference architecture for the Internet of Things. However, in the implementation of a manager utilizing ArchM, there may be changes in the relationships or usage of the entities. Figure 2 presents the conceptual model of ISO/IEC 30141:2018, showing the relationships among the entities present in IoT solutions.

The colored classes represent the elements deemed relevant from the conceptual model of ISO/IEC 30141:2018 for management in ArchM. In Figure 2, these elements are highlighted in distinct colors, allowing for clear visual identification. The meanings of each of these elements are presented below:

  • Green: represents the physical entities that may be managed.
  • Yellow: corresponds to the software used for management, always considering the separation between functional and management software.
  • Blue: related to all forms of storage for management information.
  • Orange: refers to the management platform based on ArchM that may exist within Smart Solutions.

The architectural elements defined for management in ArchM adhere to some of the guidelines established in ISO/IEC 30141:2018. These elements include:

  • Gateway: responsible for connecting devices to the network or the internet, as well as abstracting the heterogeneity of the devices.
  • Device: a hardware component that may consist of sensors and/or actuators.
  • Sensor: a hardware component used to capture environmental information.
  • Actuator: a hardware component responsible for making modifications to the environment in which it is situated.
  • Services¹: a software component tasked with data capture, environmental interaction, and communication.

¹ The minimum management is performed on the services (software) existing within an IoT infrastructure, as the primary focus is on the management of devices.

The Smart City Solution, or Smart Solution (SS), is an IoT solution developed to address a specific need of a Smart City, such as vehicle traffic solutions, smart parking, or smart buildings. Each Smart City Solution consists of the following entities: devices, gateways, sensors, actuators, services, and data storage.

The Smart Manager (SM) refers to any service used for managing the entities within a Smart City Solution, which forms part of the management platform based on ArchM. It is important to note that not all solutions in a Smart City have an SM, as shown in Figure 3.

3. Device Layer

The device layer encompasses all types of physical assets that can be managed within an IoT infrastructure. In the ArchM, a device is defined as any hardware component connected to a Smart Solution that requires management.

In the ArchM, gateways, sensors, and actuators are considered basic devices for management purposes. Additionally, a device can be composed of these basic devices. In this situation, the composite component is treated as a single unit for management purposes.

Figure 6 - Types of Devices and Organization

3.1 Gateway

Gateways are robust and complex devices equipped with significant processing power and storage capacity compared to simpler devices like sensors and actuators. As illustrated in Figure 6, gateways serve to connect devices that lack direct communication capability with the Internet.

In the context of ArchM, as shown in Figure 6, gateways are responsible for executing functions that involve a certain degree of complexity. This enables the creation of functionality sets that can be distributed throughout a Smart City. The management resources that may exist in a gateway are described below:

  • Communication: The gateway is responsible for providing resources that allow devices without direct means of connecting to the infrastructure to communicate. Thus, the gateway acts as a bridge, mapping the device's communication protocol to an Internet communication protocol.
  • Data Processing: Due to its greater processing capacity, the gateway can offer resources to process the management data received from devices before sending it. This includes treatments such as aggregation and filtering, among others.
  • Service Execution: The gateway is also used to execute resources, allowing for the distribution of management services across various gateways within a Smart Solution.
  • Device Control: This functionality enables the control of devices connected to the gateway, allowing for remote changes, such as firmware updates, activation or deactivation of devices, and other operations.
  • Storage: The storage capacity of gateways is utilized to persist the most recent management data for two purposes: first, to allow accumulated data to be sent in a single message; and second, to ensure that only relevant data is sent.

3.2 Sensor

Any device that has the ability to capture data from the environment in which it is placed for use by the Smart Solution is considered a sensor in the ArchM. Sensors can vary in type, but generally, they are simple devices and have different forms of connectivity. In the ArchM, the sensor is a basic device that is always connected to a gateway, as illustrated in Figure 6.

3.3 Actuator

The actuator is a device that has the ability to make changes to the physical environment in which it is placed. Like sensors, actuators can vary in type and have different forms of communication. Although they may be more complex than sensors, they still require a gateway to connect to the internet, as shown in Figure 6.

3.4 Protocols for Device Communication

The devices present in IoT solutions, including those used in Smart Cities, exhibit a wide variety of characteristics, such as different communication, processing, and storage capabilities. This diversity creates the need to adopt methods that make these differences irrelevant during the development of platforms for managing such devices.

Therefore, it is necessary to utilize protocols that adapt to the characteristics of the devices and are highly reliable to ensure efficient communication. Traditional protocols, such as those used on the web, may not be suitable for IoT devices. Below, we present some protocols that can be utilized by IoT devices:

Constrained Application Protocol (CoAP): Designed for constrained devices (with low energy, memory, and computing capabilities), such as sensor nodes, allowing the capture of values read by the devices. It utilizes the UDP protocol and supports multicast for IoT applications [1].

Message Queuing Telemetry Transport (MQTT): An open messaging protocol based on the publish/subscribe paradigm, used for lightweight communications. MQTT employs a client/server model, collecting data from sensors and sending it to a server (broker) [2].

Advanced Message Queuing Protocol (AMQP): An application layer protocol used to transfer information between applications or organizations. AMQP transmits data as a byte stream and supports reliability, flow control, authentication, among others [3].

The Accessible Thing Universe - TATU: A protocol that extends MQTT, standardizing messages for communication between devices and gateways [4].

Figure 7 - Device Layer and Communication

These are examples of protocols used in the device layer. Thus, for the architecture development, it was necessary to define a layer that supports various protocols. Figure 7 illustrates the device layer and its interaction with the communication layer.

Figure 7 illustrates some of the communication methods that devices can use within a Smart Solution. It is noted that ArchM does not impose restrictions on the communication methods of the devices, which is an essential feature in the context of Smart Cities due to the diversity of devices employed in different solutions.

3.5 Device Communication in ArchM

ArchM will be applied in the creation of management platforms for IoT solutions in different scenarios within Smart Cities. This will standardize communication between various solutions, facilitating the sharing of management information. To achieve this goal, the communication protocol must implement appropriate message structures. Figure 8 presents an example of how standardization in communication between a sensor and an IoT gateway can be achieved.

As illustrated in Figure 8, a device initiates its communication with the gateway (which allows its connection to the internet) by sending its basic management information, essential for identification and registration. Additionally, the device also transmits sensory data along with the management data. This approach takes advantage of the opportunity to send both sensory and management data in a single packet, optimizing transmission.

In a second instance, only the sensory data is sent, accompanied by the sensor identifier. With this identifier, it is possible to recognize which devices are connected to the gateway and fully operational. In the first transmission, the device also includes a timer, indicating the need to send information every 1000 milliseconds. This allows the gateway to detect the device's disconnection if it does not send data within this time interval.

Figure 8 - Device Communication

There is a continuous need for communication between the gateway and the device. Therefore, the gateway must be capable of sending commands to the connected devices. It is recommended to use protocols such as TATU, which extends MQTT and standardizes communication messages between IoT devices and gateways, using JSON notation.

Figure 9 - Device Operations

The TATU protocol offers three specific methods for interacting with devices:

  • GET: Collects real-time information from the device, without a defined periodicity.
  • SET: Allows for the alteration of device settings, as defined by another component.
  • FLOW: Requests data to be sent periodically every N seconds.

These operations allow for both the transmission of management information and the execution of management commands.

Figure 9 illustrates the dynamics of executing the TATU protocol. It presents three distinct moments, each related to one of the mentioned operations, considering that the device is already properly connected to the gateway.

In Figure 9, the gateway makes a GET request, asking for the type of sensor, as seen in the device's response. In addition to the sensor type, the response also includes the ID and the value of the sensor, taking the opportunity to send this management information during regular communication.

In the second moment, a SET request is made, which, although not detailed in Figure 9, is processed by the device without returning a result. This could represent an internal operation, such as temporarily disabling the device, for example.

Finally, the FLOW operation will request that management parameters be sent to the gateway every time the sensor information is transmitted. However, if no information is sent within a predefined time interval, a specific message should be triggered, containing only the requested management data. This approach ensures that the gateway continues to receive critical management data even when sensor data is not transmitted regularly.

4. Communication Layer

The communication layer is responsible for managing all traffic of managerial information. To achieve this, it must be capable of supporting the various forms of communication possible in an IoT solution for Smart Cities. Figure 10 presents the main modules of this layer and their interactions with the other components of the architecture.

In Figure 10, it can be observed that the communication layer is composed of two main modules: the communication module and the action module. The communication module is responsible for providing interfaces that allow communication between different devices using their respective protocols. In turn, the action module is responsible for enabling access from the various layers to the devices.

4.1 Communication Module

As the module responsible for communication between different types of devices, this module must implement interfaces for each communication protocol used in the device layer. This allows for management operations such as GET, SET, and FLOW to be performed for any communication protocol.

Figure 11 - Communication and actuation modules

Figure 11 details the interaction of the communication layer with the device, monitoring, maintenance, and intervention layers. First, devices send data through their standard protocols. Then, the communication module, which provides communication interfaces—represented by the MQTT and AMQP modules—captures the messages. Finally, the module extracts the management data, assembles a message in the appropriate format, and sends it to the monitoring layer. The same process occurs in the reverse direction, meaning from the upper layers to the devices.

Figure 12 demonstrates the message conversion operation. Initially, the message contains a variety of information, including sensing and management data. After passing through the communication layer, the message is altered. In the modified message, only the necessary management information for the monitoring layer is included at the second stage.

Figure 12 - Sending a message with management data

4.2 Action Module

The action module is responsible for enabling the other layers to communicate with the devices. As shown in Figure 10, in the ArchM, the monitoring, maintenance, and intervention layers are directly connected to the communication layer.

Given the wide variety of existing devices, the communication module is also tasked with handling the conversion of commands sent by the monitoring, maintenance, and intervention layers to the devices. Thus, when the command reaches the action module, it is converted to the standard and communication format accepted by the device.

Figure 13 - Sending command to device

The Figure 13 presents the sequence of steps taken to execute a command sent to the communication layer. After sending a command to the device, the communication layer identifies the command and the device responsible for its execution. Then, it performs the conversion of the command, if necessary, to the standard accepted by the device. When the device receives the command, it is executed.

4.3 Communication Standard

As described, communication can be carried out through different protocols. However, it will be necessary to implement basic routines for communication in the context of management for each protocol used. From this point, a generic overview will be provided on how each of the basic communication operations should be conducted within a Smart Solution.

The operations can be divided into two categories: requests, which include two types—data request (GET) and flow request (FLOW) — and modifications (SET), which perform alteration operations on devices.

The GET operation is responsible for requesting management data from devices. This data can be sent in standard sensing messages, either opportunistically or not. However, there may be a variation of the GET request for messages to be sent immediately. Figure 14 details the execution of the management data request from a server to a device.

Figure 14 - Sending command from server to device

In Figure 14, each step of the execution is numbered, and the messages are detailed. Initially, a management server issues a GET request. It identifies the following parameters: 'type_request', 'id_gateway', 'id_device', 'command', and 'immediate'.
  • type_request: indicates the type of request being made to the device (GET, FLOW, SET).
  • id_gateway: contains the identification of the gateway that connects the device to the Internet (depending on the Smart Solution, this parameter may be omitted).
  • id_device: contains the identifier of the device that is to execute the command.
  • command: identifies the command to be executed by the device (RESET, TIME_ON, STATUS, INFO).
  • immediate: specifies how the response should be handled, indicating whether the response should be embedded in a sensing message or sent as a separate message.

The communication layer intercepts the command sent from the server at the second stage. At this point, the message is converted into the format accepted by the device and then forwarded. In the third stage, there is a difference in the format of the message adapted to the device. It contains the following parameters:

  • request: identifies the type of request made to the device (GET, FLOW, POST).
  • command: this parameter contains the execution instructions for the device. It can have different variations depending on the device's protocol. In Figure 14, the command has two parameters: instruction, which specifies what is being requested, and immediate, which indicates how the response will be carried out.
  • timeout: indicates the device's response time, needed to determine whether the message was delivered and executed. If the time is exceeded, it is inferred that the message was not delivered.

In Figure 14, moment five shows the response being sent. One of the parameters in the message body contains the response data. It also includes information on the temperature (sensing data) and the device status (management data). This is due to the parameter immediate: “false”, indicating that management data should be sent along with the sensing messages, thereby reducing the number of messages on the network. The parameters in the status are described below.

  • timestamp: identifies the moment when the management data was captured.
  • mode: identifies the device's operational mode at that time (OPERATIONAL, FAILURE, TEST, MAINTENANCE).
  • flow: indicates whether the sensing data is being sent via a stream.
  • power: displays data on the device's power consumption.

At moment six, the message is converted to the Internet communication standard, with a clear separation between the management data and the sensing data. The converted message is shown in moment six of Figure 14, where the control parameters are also presented, containing only the management data requested by the server.

The FLOW operation is responsible for requesting management data from devices, similar to the GET operation. However, in this operation, a continuous flow of management data from the device is maintained at pre-determined intervals. Figure 15 presents a generic example of the FLOW operation in use.

Like the GET operation, the FLOW operation follows the same first four steps, with differences starting from step five. However, in steps one and three, a new parameter can be observed: time, which indicates the time interval for sending each message. It is important to note that, when using the FLOW operation, multiple requests are unnecessary, as management information is continuously sent.

Figure 15 - Sending the flow command from the server to the device

At moment seven, presented in Figure 15, the operation is divided into two parts. In moment 7A, after a certain amount of data is sent from the device to the communication layer — represented by moments five and six in Figure 15 — the data is aggregated into a single message to be subsequently sent to the server. In moment 7B, the aggregated management data is presented in a single message to the server. It is worth noting that the aggregation shown is a generalization of what may occur, and in an implemented platform using the logic for the FLOW operation, the aggregation, message format, and other parameters may vary.

The SET operation is designed to make modifications to devices in a Smart Solution, addressing both hardware and software aspects. On the hardware side, configurations like the state of an actuator (on/off) can be changed. On the software side, adjustments such as data collection intervals, operational modes, and firmware updates or modifications can be carried out. The SET operation, when sent from a server, is similar to the previously discussed GET and FLOW operations. Figure 16 provides a generic example of how the SET operation is used.

Figure 16 - Configuration command sent from server to device

At first, in Figure 16, it is observed that a SET request is sent, indicating that a modification should be made in a Smart Solution. Information about the gateway and the device that will execute the command is also sent; in this case, for the device shutdown.

In the second stage, similar to the GET and FLOW operations, the request is converted by the communication layer into the communication standard accepted by the device. Then, in the third stage, the converted message is sent to the device. In the converted request, it is noted that only the identification of the operation type and the details of the command to be executed are present. In the fourth stage, the command is successfully executed by the device. It is important to note that this illustration does not show details about execution errors or communication failures.

5. Monitoring

The monitoring layer, shown in Figure 17, is responsible for handling the management data of a Smart Solution coming from the communication layer. Two modules were defined for this purpose: device monitoring and data processing. The functionality of each module in this layer is briefly described below.

  • Device Monitoring: involves capturing the management data of all hardware and software assets in a Smart Solution, allowing for periodic verification of activity across the infrastructure.
  • Data Processing: is responsible for performing the necessary conversions to ensure that the data is stored optimally, enabling its use by other components.

Figure 17 - Monitoring Layer

5.1 Device Monitoring

The device monitoring module is responsible for collecting management data from the devices of a given Smart Solution. However, this data can be categorized based on the type of information it represents. In ArchM, the data is classified into hardware data and software data.

Hardware monitoring deals exclusively with information related to the hardware of each device. However, this activity is not limited to monitoring and action devices used in the Smart Solution but also includes the gateways that connect these devices to the internet. In ArchM, the following basic data is considered essential for device monitoring:

  • Gateway: id, name, manufacturer, serial number, model, power supply, battery level, total memory, free memory, total processing capacity, free processing capacity, storage capacity, MAC address, IP address, connection type, and operating system.
  • Sensing Devices: id, name, manufacturer, serial number, model, power supply, battery level, version, sensor type, unit of measurement, and measurement interval.
  • Actuating Devices: id, name, manufacturer, serial number, model, power supply, battery level, type of actuation, and actuation method.

It is worth noting that the gateway, being a more complex device, provides information such as the operating system, processing capacity, storage, and other details. Sensing devices, on the other hand, include the measurement interval attribute, which indicates the frequency of data transmission. Actuating devices have the actuation method attribute, which defines the type of modification they perform.

Software monitoring is responsible for managing information related to the software present on gateways, allowing the monitoring of the type, version, and function of each software. The basic data regarding software management in a Smart Solution includes: developer, version, software type, and execution location. This category encompasses both the firmware of the devices and the software installed on gateways. The management data presented here are not intended to limit what should be monitored, but rather to provide guidance, allowing for modifications according to specific needs.

Figure 18 - Management data transmission

The monitoring of this information is simplified in Figure 18 through a sequence diagram illustrating the transmission and processing of device management data. Initially, the device sends its management information to the communication layer, which, as described earlier, performs the conversion and/or filtering of the message, if necessary. Subsequently, the data is sent to the monitoring layer. In this layer, the data is redirected to the appropriate module, either hardware or software, where it will be processed for storage purposes. Additionally, the data captured by the monitoring layer can be requested before being stored, for instance, for applications requiring real-time processing.

Figure 19 - Data Processing

5.2 Data Processing

In Figure 17, there is a dedicated module for data processing. This module is responsible for making the necessary modifications to the received monitoring data to optimize both processing and storage.

This module can execute routines such as filtering management data, altering the format of the received data, aggregating information, and adding metadata. Its significance lies in the ability to adapt management data from different sources and varied standards. The data processing should be tailored to the system's needs, and this module may be omitted or incorporated into other modules as the context requires.

Figure 20 - Example of Data Conversion

Figure 19 illustrates a diagram of a generic routine for data processing. It shows that the data received by the monitoring module is forwarded to the processing module, which applies the necessary modifications to optimize storage. Initially, a filtering of the data is performed to ensure that only relevant information for monitoring the Smart Solution is retained. Subsequently, the data can undergo two types of conversion: the first aimed at local storage and the second for remote storage.

Figure 20 presents an example of data processing. In the first step, the management data received by the management module is forwarded for processing. It can be observed that there is a set of data from a specific device, collected at different times.

The data received by the processing module is processed and then aggregated, divided, and used to construct different messages. In steps three and four, the data is stored in two distinct databases, each with the appropriate format for its respective use.

6. Maintenance

The maintenance layer is responsible for performing actions on the devices of a Smart Solution, which can be classified as preventive or corrective. Preventive maintenance involves making modifications to a device or a group of devices to ensure the continuity of the solution's operation. On the other hand, corrective maintenance aims to address issues that are compromising the full functionality of the Smart Solution.

Figure 21 - Maintenance Layer

This layer defines the necessary routines to perform corrections or maintenance on the devices and software. Routines such as rebooting, modifying software configurations, locking devices, and running simulations must be clearly defined for each type of device, considering their distinct characteristics and resources.

In Figure 21, it can be seen that the maintenance layer is related to the storage component. This is due to the need to access the characteristics and configurations of the devices being maintained. Additionally, the maintenance layer connects to the communication layer and the devices because of the requirement to transfer configuration and modification commands to the devices integrated into the Smart Solution.

The maintenance layer, as illustrated in Figure 21, consists of three fundamental modules:

  1. Hardware and Software Maintenance: Responsible for defining the basic operations that can be performed on the hardware and/or software assets within a Smart Solution.
  2. Device Control: Aims to manage the manipulation routines that can be executed on each device for maintenance purposes.
  3. Device Access: Deals with the access protocols for the devices available in a Smart Solution.

6.1 Hardware and Software Maintenance

This module is responsible for managing the operations that can be performed on the assets of a Smart Solution, defining which actions are feasible for each asset to be maintained.

As illustrated in Figure 21, the hardware and software maintenance module is interconnected with the device access module and the storage layer. By accessing the management data, this module identifies the operations that can be executed on the asset or the Smart Solution in question.

Figure 22 presents the sequence diagram of the hardware and software maintenance module. In the first moment, there is a request from an entity to perform a maintenance action, which can originate from a higher layer or an external software. Next, the hardware and software maintenance module is triggered to execute the requested task on the device. Before proceeding, the module checks the storage layer to see which properties are available for modification on the devices and software. After this verification, access to the device is requested. The hardware and software maintenance module then sends the request to the device control, which, in turn, forwards the request to the device. Finally, upon receiving a response, it is sent back to the entity that originated the maintenance request.

Figure 22 - Sequence diagram of the maintenance layer

6.2 Device Control

The Device Control module is responsible for managing the actions performed by each device in Smart Solutions during the maintenance process. Through this module, the maintenance layer sends commands to make changes to existing devices. In this context, the requested command may be converted into a more specific format that is understood by the device.

Figure 22 illustrates how the Device Control module operates when executing commands on a device. It shows that the control module receives a request and forwards it to the device where the action is to be performed. The module then waits and returns a response after the command has been executed.

6.3 Device Access

This module is responsible for verifying connection permissions and access to the requested functionalities. It is important to emphasize that this module focuses exclusively on access control within the proposed architecture and does not cover all security routines that may be implemented.

7. Analysis

The analysis layer encompasses a set of functionalities responsible for providing an overview of a Smart Solution. This layer processes management data to monitor a set of assets within a Smart Solution or a Smart Composition, thereby presenting the functional status of the infrastructure. The analysis layer uses various modules, which are shown in Figure 23.

Figure 23 - Analysis Layer

7.1 Device Analysis in Smart Solution

The Device Analysis module in a Smart Solution is responsible for generating an overview of the managed device infrastructure. To achieve this, it leverages the data retrieval module from the storage component, which allows access to the necessary data to assess the current state of the infrastructure. The Device Analysis module also utilizes two other modules: the Data Processing module and the View Generator. Figure 24 illustrates a possible execution sequence of the Device Analysis module in a Smart Solution.

Figure 24 - Sequence diagram highlighting the device analysis module in the Smart Solution

In the diagram, it is observed that the Device Analysis module in the Smart Solution interacts with other modules and layers. Initially, an entity, which may be a manager or software, requests an analysis, specifying which set of assets should be analyzed.

Next, the Device Analysis module retrieves the necessary management data for monitoring the requested assets. In Figure 24, the data request is directed to the storage module, which may include historical data stored on devices distributed across the infrastructure.

After collecting the necessary data to compose the current infrastructure scenario, the analysis module may request data formatting from the Data Processing module. This is due to the diversity of sources from which the data is collected. Although the data may have already been processed, there is a risk that it is not in a compatible format for processing.

The Device Analysis module may also perform some data structuring after standardization, depending on the needs of the View Generator module. Finally, the View Generator will create a general representation of the selected infrastructure and return the result to the analysis module, which, in turn, will send the response to the requesting entity.

Figure 25 - Example of data processing in the analysis layer

7.2 Data Processing

The Data Processing module is responsible for standardizing the data used in the analysis, as illustrated in Figure 23. The diagram presented in Figure 24 shows that the Data Processing module may or may not be utilized during the analysis, which applies to all modules.

In addition to standardizing the data, this module is also responsible for data cleansing, if necessary. This involves the elimination of duplicate, incomplete, or any other inconsistent records. Thus, depending on the types of data structures used, the Data Processing module can be implemented in a customized manner to handle specific structures.

Similar to the example presented in Figure 20, this module can remove irrelevant data for the analysis. Figure 25 illustrates an example of data processing, where the Device Analysis module sends a dataset—presented separately in the figure for illustrative purposes (moments 1 and 1A). After sending the data, the processing module performs conversion using the implemented logic. Subsequently, the processed data is returned to the Device Analysis module. In Figure 25, it can be observed that data processing results in the elimination of information considered irrelevant for the analysis (moment 2).

7.3 View Generator

The View Generator module is responsible for creating an overview of the analyzed infrastructure. It receives ready-to-use data, collected by the Device Analysis module, allowing the View Generator to adopt a diagnostic analysis2 approach to obtain a comprehensive panorama of the infrastructure. Additionally, this module can employ descriptive analyses3 to understand what occurred during operations, thus facilitating data visualization.

Figure 26 - Example of View Generator

In the context of ArchM, the analysis layer can utilize one or even both forms of analysis presented. Figure 26 illustrates, in a generic way, how this module can be executed. It can be observed that the management data of the devices are stored and retrieved by the analysis layer. The visualization module uses the processed data retrieved by the Device Analysis and Data Processing modules to generate a representation of the infrastructure. Finally, a file containing the analysis results is generated, which can be stored and/or presented graphically, for example.

Notes:

  • 2: Diagnostic Analysis: Using deep learning techniques to identify root causes of issues.
  • 3: Descriptive Analysis: Through the automatic recognition of patterns in large datasets

7.4 Device Analysis in Smart Composition

This module has the same functionality as the Device Analysis module in the Smart Solution. However, it is not limited to analyzing just one Smart Solution but rather a set of them, meaning a Smart Composition. To perform this task, the Device Analysis module in the Smart Composition utilizes the analysis modules of the Smart Solutions from each managed environment. Additionally, some auxiliary modules are employed, as shown in Figure 23: the Data Sharing module, the Information Aggregator, and the View Generator.

Figure 27 provides an overview of the operation of the analysis module in the Smart Composition. It can be observed that each Smart Solution performs its analyses individually, while the analysis module in the Smart Composition is responsible for coordinating the assembly of an aggregated view of the overall situation.

Figure 27 - Overview of Device Analysis in Smart Composition

7.5 Information Aggregator

The Information Aggregator is an auxiliary module, as illustrated in Figure 23, that is used by the modules performing analysis in the Smart Composition. It is responsible for organizing all the information received from the Smart Solutions and enabling the joint analysis of these data.

Figure 28 - Fault Identification Layer

In Figure 27, the Information Aggregator is presented as the module that receives analysis data from the Smart Solutions. This function allows it to perform tasks such as standardization, deletion, and correction of data, similar to data cleansing during the analysis. It is important to note that the Device Analysis module must coordinate the actions of this module in the Smart Composition.

8. Fault Identification Layer

This layer aims to utilize the captured management data to identify potential anomalies that may occur in the Smart Solution. Any situation that interferes with the operation of the infrastructure is considered an anomaly. Examples include hardware overload, delays in software execution, and slow network connections. These parameters can be defined as needed within a platform.

Figure 29 - Execution sequence of the fault identification module for devices in the smart solution

Figure 28 presents the organization of the Fault Identification Layer, showing its modules and the relationships between them and the other layers. It can be observed that there are two main modules and two auxiliary modules, along with interactions with the modules of the storage component and the analysis layer.

8.1 Fault Identification in Devices within the Smart Solution

The Fault Identification module is responsible for detecting potential failures that may occur in a Smart Solution. It coordinates the retrieval of relevant data and requests the current status of the Smart Solution, as shown in Figure 28, through connections with the storage and analysis layer modules.

The diagram in Figure 29 presents a generic execution sequence of this module. Initially, there is a request made to the Fault Identification module. Subsequently, the module requests the status of the devices from the analysis layer, specifically from the Device Analysis module in the Smart Solution. After this step, the module retrieves historical data to assist in identifying failures.

Next, the strategy analysis module defines which possible actions (scenarios) can be executed to resolve the issue. Finally, the scenario generator determines which scenario will be used, as only one of the scenarios will be chosen as the potential cause of the failures in the Smart Solution. It is important to note that this scenario can be predefined, which would exclude these steps.

8.2 Strategy Analyzer

The Strategy Analyzer module is utilized by all fault identification modules, as shown in Figure 28. This module is responsible for using the data transferred by the identification modules to define which actions can be taken to correct the failure. For example, if the fault identification module determines that a gateway is experiencing a high delay in sending information, the Strategy Analyzer module must identify different sets of actions that can resolve the gateway issue.

In Figure 30, it can be observed that this module may not need to create actions to correct the failure but can rely on a set of pre-established strategies. In this situation, the module simply forwards which sets of actions can be executed to the scenario generator module. However, there is nothing preventing the implementation of a solution where actions are generated dynamically.

8.3 Scenario Generator

After the actions to be taken in response to a failure are selected, the scenario generator module will be responsible for defining which action or scenario will be executed to correct the problem.

In Figure 30, the scenario generator presents the possible actions generated or pre-established by the Strategy Analyzer module. Next to each action, there is a percentage indicating which one (scenario) has the highest probability of resolving the issue.

8.4 Fault Identification in Devices within the Smart Composition

The Fault Identification module for devices in the Smart Composition is responsible for detecting potential failures that may occur within a Smart Composition. This module considers not only a single Smart Solution but a set of them.

The execution sequence of this module is presented in the diagram of Figure 31.

Figure 30 - Overview of the use of the strategy analyzer and scenario generator modules

First, a request is made by an entity to execute fault identification in the infrastructure. Next, the module will request a set of data from the analysis layer and the storage layer. With this information, the module will identify the potential issue occurring and relay this information to the Strategy Analyzer module, which will propose actions to correct the failure.

Figure 31 - Execution sequence of the device failure prediction module in the smart composition

9. Intervention Layer

The Intervention Layer is responsible for managing the necessary modifications in the Smart Solutions to ensure the proper functioning of the infrastructure. Figure 32 presents the modules of the Intervention Layer and the relationships between them. For simplification, the relationships of the modules with other layers have been omitted from the figure.

The Intervention Layer consists of two main modules and three auxiliary modules. The main modules are: Intervention in Devices within the Smart Solution and Intervention in Devices within the Smart Composition. These modules are responsible for managing all the necessary steps to carry out the intervention. The auxiliary modules, which handle specific tasks, include: Intervention Generator, Intervention Analyzer, and Maintenance by Intervention.

Figure 32 - Intervention Layer

9.1 Intervention in Devices the Smart Solution

This module is responsible for implementing modifications to prevent and correct failures or low performance in the Smart Solution infrastructure. To achieve this, it utilizes the auxiliary modules Intervention Generator and Intervention Analyzer, in addition to interacting with components from the storage, fault identification, and maintenance layers. This interaction allows for data retrieval and access to the necessary devices, optimizing the intervention in the Smart Solution.

Figure 33 - Execution sequence of the intervention module for devices in the smart solution

Figure 33 presents the sequence diagram of a possible execution of the Intervention module. It can be seen that the intervention request is made by the Fault Identification layer. The Intervention module then retrieves data from the Fault Identification layer, potentially requesting additional data from the storage layer, depending on the implementation and the sufficiency of the existing information.

After collecting the data, the Intervention module activates the Intervention Generator and the Intervention Analyzer to propose a potential intervention in the infrastructure of the devices within the Smart Solution. It is important to highlight that, although the Fault Identification layer suggests an action, only the Intervention layer can verify the feasibility of executing that correction. Finally, the resulting configuration is passed to the Maintenance by Intervention module, which implements the necessary changes in the devices.

9.2 Intervention Generator

The Intervention Generator module is responsible for adapting the scenario identified by the Problem Identification layer to execute interventions in Smart Solutions and Smart Compositions. The intervention is based on the information from the Fault Identification layer, considering the specificities of each solution. The Intervention Generator checks whether the configurations suggested as corrective actions can be applied directly or if they need adjustments due to the infrastructure's particularities.

Figure 34 provides an overview of the execution of the Intervention Generator module. It shows how the data obtained from the Fault Identification layer is used by the Intervention Generator, which then produces the best possible configurations based on the specific characteristics of the infrastructure. Finally, this information is sent to the Intervention Analysis module.

Figure 34 - Overview of the intervention generator and intervention analysis modules

9.3 Intervention Analysis

The Intervention Analysis Module is responsible for selecting the best configuration for the Smart Solution and/or Smart Composition. To achieve this, it must take into account the capacity for changes that can be made, as well as the permissions for the possible modifications. For instance, a device may be running a critical process that cannot be interrupted or transferred to another device.

Figure 35 - Execution sequence of the intervention module in devices within a smart composition

In Figure 34, it is observed that the Intervention Analysis Module receives information about the possible configurations from the Intervention Generator Module. Then, it performs database checks to evaluate the permissions and privileges that allow for the implementation of the configuration. Based on this information, the Intervention Analysis Module selects the best possible configuration, considering the current state of the infrastructure.

9.4 Intervention on Devices in the Smart Composition

The Device Intervention Module handles the modifications that need to be made in the Smart Composition to address the problems identified by the Fault Identification Layer. It leverages auxiliary components like the Intervention Generator and Intervention Analysis. Additionally, it uses the Storage Layer to retrieve data that assists in defining the intervention approach, as well as the Fault Identification and Maintenance modules to carry out the necessary modifications. It’s important to note that communication with the devices is conducted through another module, called Maintenance Intervention.

Figure 35 presents the execution sequence of the Device Intervention Module. In it, we see a request made by an entity, which would be the Problem Identification Layer. The module then requests the necessary data from the Problem Identification and Storage layers. From this point, the Intervention Generator and Intervention Analysis modules define the best strategy for modifying the devices in the Smart Composition. Finally, the Maintenance Intervention Layer communicates with the Smart Composition to implement the necessary modifications.

9.5 Maintenance by Intervention

The Maintenance Intervention Module is responsible for managing communication between the Intervention Layer and the Maintenance Layer to implement intervention configurations in Smart Solutions. To achieve this, the module must coordinate the transmission of configuration data to each Smart Solution, taking into account the communication characteristics and specific standards of each solution.

10. Storage Layer

All storage resources utilized in the ArchM are considered part of the storage layer, which encompasses everything from cloud databases to temporary storage on simple devices. Thus, the storage layer is responsible for coordinating the storage and retrieval of management data within the infrastructure.

Figure 36 presents the components of the storage layer and their relationships. This layer consists of three modules: data storage management, data retrieval management, and data sharing. The Storage Base Search module is responsible for the logic of searching for data distributed across different databases. It can be implemented as a separate module or integrated into another module, depending on the chosen approach. It is important to emphasize that, in an implementation of the ArchM, some functionalities of these modules may be executed by existing solutions in storage services.

Figure 36 - Storage Layer

10.1 Management Data Storage

The management data storage module is responsible for receiving management information from the monitoring layer and appropriately storing it in various databases that may be in use. This module also uses the Storage Base Search to coordinate the distributed storage of the received management information. Although not depicted in Figure 36, other layers may utilize this module to store data.

10.2 Management Data Recover

This module is responsible for querying the distributed management data and returning it as a set of information formatted according to the requester’s needs. To do this, it uses the Storage Base Search module to retrieve the distributed data and the data sharing module to meet the specific characteristics of the requester.

10.3 Data Sharing

The data sharing module addresses issues related to the use of management data by different management solutions. For example, the management data produced by a Smart Solution can be used to select intervention strategies in a Smart Composition. Additionally, the data sharing module manages the logic of external access to management data, allowing third-party applications that are not directly linked to the Smart Solution to access this information.

10.4 Storage Base Search

The Storage Base Search, as presented in Figure 36, is not considered a native module, since its functionalities will likely be performed by existing technological solutions. However, it is responsible for determining where the management data will be stored and what format will be used, taking into account the needs for managing the infrastructure.
